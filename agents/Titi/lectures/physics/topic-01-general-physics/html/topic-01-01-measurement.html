1.1.1 Scalars & Vectors
📖 Definition

Scalar — a quantity with magnitude only (size, no direction).

Vector — a quantity with both magnitude and direction.

Scalars Vectors
Mass, Time, Speed, Distance,
Energy, Temperature, Volume		 Force, Velocity, Acceleration,
Momentum, Weight, Displacement
💡 Key Point: A vector is represented by an arrow. The length of the arrow shows the magnitude; the arrowhead shows the direction.
💡 Key Point

Distance vs Displacement:

• Distance (scalar) — total path length travelled.

• Displacement (vector) — straight-line distance from start to end, with direction.

⚠️ Common Mistake
❌ "Speed and velocity are the same thing."
✅ Speed is scalar (magnitude only). Velocity is a vector (magnitude + direction). A car going around a roundabout at 30 km/h has constant speed but changing velocity!
📝 Worked Example

A person walks 3 m east, then 4 m north.

Step 1: Distance = 3 + 4 = 7 m
Step 2: Displacement = √(3² + 4²) = 5 m at 53° north of east
Distance = 7 m (scalar) | Displacement = 5 m NE (vector)
✅ Quick Check
Q1: Which of these is a vector? (a) 10 kg (b) 10 m/s north (c) 10°C
Answer: (b) 10 m/s north — has magnitude + direction.
Q2: Is "energy" a scalar or vector?
Answer: Scalar — it has magnitude only, no direction.
1.1.2 Length Measurement
📖 Instruments & Precision
Instrument Precision Measures
📏 Metre Ruler ±1 mm Lengths > 10 cm
🔧 Vernier Caliper ±0.1 mm Small lengths, diameter, depth
🔩 Micrometer Screw Gauge ±0.01 mm Thin wires, small spheres
💡 Key Point
Parallax Error: When reading a scale, your eye must be perpendicular (straight on) to the marking. Reading from an angle gives a false reading.
Zero Error: When the instrument reads non-zero when the jaws are fully closed. This reading must be subtracted (or added) from every measurement.
⚠️ Common Mistake
❌ Reading a micrometre but forgetting to check the zero error first.
✅ Always check and record the zero error before measuring. If the reading is +0.02 mm when closed, subtract 0.02 mm from every measurement.
📝 Worked Example

A student measures the diameter of a wire using a micrometer. The main scale reads 4.5 mm and the thimble scale reads 22. The micrometer has a zero error of −0.01 mm.

Step 1: Raw reading = 4.5 + (22 × 0.01) = 4.72 mm
Step 2: Corrected reading = 4.72 − (−0.01) = 4.73 mm
Corrected diameter = 4.73 mm
✅ Quick Check
Q1: Which instrument gives the most precise measurement of a wire's diameter?
Answer: Micrometer screw gauge (±0.01 mm).
Q2: If a vernier caliper has a zero error of +0.1 mm, and you measure a reading of 3.7 mm, what is the actual length?
Answer: 3.6 mm (3.7 − 0.1 = 3.6 mm).
1.1.3 Time Measurement
📖 Instruments
Instrument Precision Best used for
⏱️ Stopwatch (manual) ±0.1 s (human reaction) Timing experiments, pendulum
🔦 Light Gate + Data Logger ±0.001 s Speed/acceleration of trolleys
🔢 Formula
T = 2π √( L / g )

Period of a simple pendulum: T = time for one complete swing, L = length, g = gravitational field strength.

💡 Key Point
Light gates are more accurate than stopwatches because they eliminate human reaction time error. The data logger records the exact moment the beam is interrupted.
📝 Worked Example

A pendulum of length 0.4 m takes 20 swings in 25.3 s. Find the time period.

Step 1: Time period = total time ÷ number of swings
Step 2: T = 25.3 ÷ 20 = 1.265 s
T = 1.27 s (3 s.f.)
✅ Quick Check
Q1: Why is a light gate more accurate than a stopwatch for measuring the speed of a trolley?
Answer: It removes human reaction time error — the light gate triggers automatically when the card passes through.
1.1.4 Volume & Area
📖 Measuring Volume
Object Method Formula
Regular solid Measure dimensions with ruler V = l × w × h
Irregular solid Displacement method (Eureka can) V = V₂ − V₁
Liquid Measuring cylinder Read meniscus at eye level
💡 Key Point
Meniscus: When reading a measuring cylinder, the water surface is curved. Read the bottom of the meniscus at eye level.
⚠️ Common Mistake
❌ Reading the top of the meniscus instead of the bottom.
✅ The bottom of the curved surface is the true volume. Always read at eye level to avoid parallax.
📝 Worked Example

A stone is placed in a Eureka can filled with water. The water displaced is collected in a measuring cylinder. Initial reading = 40 mL, final reading = 67 mL.

Step 1: Volume of stone = volume of water displaced
Step 2: V = 67 − 40 = 27 mL = 27 cm³
Volume of stone = 27 cm³
✅ Quick Check
Q1: A rectangular block has length 5 cm, width 3 cm, height 2 cm. Find its volume.
Answer: V = 5 × 3 × 2 = 30 cm³
Q2: Why is the displacement method better than a ruler for finding the volume of a pebble?
Answer: A pebble is irregularly shaped — a ruler cannot measure its volume directly.
📌 Section 1.1 Summary
🔑 Formula Sheet
T = 2π √( L / g )   |   V = l × w × h   |   V = V₂ − V₁
📌 Key Takeaways

1. Scalar = magnitude only. Vector = magnitude + direction.

2. Micrometre (±0.01 mm) > Vernier caliper (±0.1 mm) > Ruler (±1 mm) in precision.

3. Zero error must be checked before every measurement.

4. Parallax error is avoided by reading at eye level, perpendicular to the scale.

5. Irregular solids → displacement method (Eureka can).

🧪 Key Experiment: Pendulum Timing

Aim: Measure the time period of a pendulum and find g.

Method:

1. Set up a pendulum with a bob and string.
2. Measure the length L from pivot to centre of bob.
3. Displace the bob slightly and release.
4. Time 20 complete swings using a stopwatch (to reduce % error).
5. T = total time ÷ 20.
6. Repeat for different lengths.

💡 Why 20 swings? — Human reaction time error is fixed (±0.2 s). Timing 20 swings spreads this error over 20 cycles, reducing the percentage error.
