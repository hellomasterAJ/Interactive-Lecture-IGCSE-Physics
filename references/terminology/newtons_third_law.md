# Newton's Third Law of Motion (Law of Action-Reaction)

## 📖 Etymology

In the original *Principia*, Newton's Third Law reads:

> *"Actioni contrariam semper et æqualem esse reactionem: sive corporum duorum actiones in se mutuo semper esse æquales et in partes contrarias dirigi."*

— "To every action there is always opposed an equal reaction: or the mutual actions of two bodies upon each other are always equal and directed to contrary parts."

The term **"action"** (*actio*) and **"reaction"** (*reactio*) were chosen by Newton to emphasise the **mutual interaction** between two bodies. In modern physics, we prefer **"force pair"** or **"interaction pair"** to avoid the misleading implication that one force happens "before" the other.

---

## 📜 The History Behind the Third Law

### Before Newton

The idea of equal and opposite forces was not obvious to earlier thinkers:

- **Aristotle** believed that a mover imparts force to an object, but did not consider the object pushing back
- **Descartes** (1644) proposed a crude version in his rules of collision, but they were largely incorrect
- **Huygens** (1669) correctly analysed elastic collisions and proved that relative speed is conserved — but did not generalise to a universal law

### Newton's Insight

Newton realised that the Third Law was not just about collisions — it was a **universal principle** applying to all forces: gravity, contact forces, magnetism, and tension. He used it to derive:
- **Conservation of momentum** (as a corollary of the Third Law)
- **The motion of the centre of mass** of a system
- **Tidal forces** between the Earth and the Moon

### The Importance of "Different Objects"

The critical and most misunderstood aspect: action and reaction forces act on **two different objects**. They never act on the same object, and therefore **they never cancel each other**.

## 📐 The Law

### Modern Statement

> **When one object exerts a force on a second object, the second object exerts an equal and opposite force on the first.**

$$ \boxed{\vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA}} $$

| Notation | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| $\vec{F}_{AB}$ | Force on object A **from** object B |
| $\vec{F}_{BA}$ | Force on object B **from** object A |
| Equal magnitude | $|F_{AB}| = |F_{BA}|$ |
| Opposite direction | $\vec{F}_{AB}$ and $\vec{F}_{BA}$ point in opposite directions |

### Three Essential Facts About Force Pairs

1. **They are equal in magnitude** — no exceptions
2. **They are opposite in direction** — always
3. **They act on different objects** — this is the key!

### Examples of Force Pairs

| Situation | Action Force | Reaction Force |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Book on a table** | Book pushes DOWN on table ($F_{BT}$) | Table pushes UP on book ($F_{TB}$) |
| **Walking** | Foot pushes BACK on ground ($F_{FG}$) | Ground pushes FORWARD on foot ($F_{GF}$) |
| **Rocket** | Engine pushes EXHAUST DOWN ($F_{RE}$) | Exhaust pushes ROCKET UP ($F_{ER}$) |
| **Tug of war** | Team A pulls LEFT on rope ($F_{AR}$) | Rope pulls RIGHT on Team A ($F_{RA}$) |
| **Gravity (Earth–Moon)** | Earth pulls Moon ($F_{EM}$) | Moon pulls Earth ($F_{ME}$) |

## 🧪 Key Experiment — The Spring Balance Pair

**Setup**: Two spring balances are hooked together.

**Procedure**: Pull on one balance. Observe both readings.

**Result**: **Both balances read exactly the same number** — regardless of which one you pull harder.

**Why**: Balance A exerts a force on balance B. Balance B exerts an equal and opposite force on balance A. The readings are always identical.

## 🌍 Everyday Examples

| Situation | Third Law in Action |
|-----------|--------------------|
| 🏊 **Swimming** | You push water backward → water pushes you forward |
| 🚀 **Rocket propulsion** | Exhaust gases pushed downward → gases push rocket upward |
| 🚗 **Car driving** | Tyres push road backward → road pushes car forward |
| 🪂 **Jumping** | You push ground downward → ground pushes you upward |
| ⛸️ **Ice skating** | Push off from the wall → wall pushes you backward |
| 🔫 **Recoil of a gun** | Gun pushes bullet forward → bullet pushes gun backward (recoil) |
| 🪰 **A bird flying** | Wings push air downward → air pushes bird upward |

## 💡 Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Truth |
|--------------|-------|
| "Action and reaction forces cancel each other out" | ❌ They act on **different objects**, so they cannot cancel. To find the net force on one object, you only consider forces on **that** object |
| "The action comes before the reaction" | ❌ The forces occur **simultaneously** — there is no "first" force. Newton used the word "action" to mean "interaction" |
| "A larger object exerts a larger force than a smaller one" | ❌ Newton's Third Law says the forces are **always equal**, regardless of size. A car and a mosquito exert equal forces on each other in a collision — the mosquito just has much less mass to resist |
| "The Third Law only applies to contact forces" | ❌ It applies to **all forces** — gravity, magnetism, electric forces — any interaction between two objects |
| "If the Earth pulls on me, I don't pull back on the Earth" | ❌ You absolutely do! $F_{\text{Earth on you}} = F_{\text{you on Earth}}$. But Earth's mass is $6 \times 10^{24}$ kg, so your pull on Earth produces an imperceptible acceleration ($a = F/m$) |
