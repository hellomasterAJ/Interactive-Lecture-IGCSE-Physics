# Work Done

## 📖 Etymology

The word **"work"** comes from the Old English **"weorc"** (also *worc*), from Proto-Germanic **"werka-"** meaning "that which is done, something done, deed, action."

In physics, the term was given a precise mathematical meaning in the 19th century during the formalisation of **thermodynamics** and **energy conservation**. The key figures were:

- **Gustave-Gaspard Coriolis** (1829) — introduced the term **"travail"** (French for "work") as $F \times d$ in his book *Du Calcul de l'Effet des Machines*
- **James Prescott Joule** (1843) — established the **mechanical equivalent of heat**, showing that work and heat are interchangeable forms of energy
- **Hermann von Helmholtz** (1847) — used the concept of work to formulate the **law of conservation of energy**

> ⚡ **Everyday vs Physics "Work":** In everyday life, "work" means any effortful activity. In physics, **work is only done when a force causes displacement**. Holding a heavy box stationary may feel like work, but in physics, $W = 0$ if there is no movement.

## 📜 Historical Timeline

### 🏛️ Ancient & Pre-Classical

The practical understanding of work existed long before physics formalised it — levers, pulleys, and ramps were used to move heavy objects. **Archimedes** (287–212 BCE) understood the mechanical advantage of simple machines, which implicitly relies on the work principle (force × distance trade-off).

### 🔭 Galileo's Insight (1599)

In *Le Meccaniche*, Galileo analysed simple machines and showed that what is **gained in force is lost in distance** — the fundamental trade-off of work. This is the first clear statement of the principle behind work:

> *"What is gained in force is lost in speed [distance]."*

### ⚙️ The Formalisation — Coriolis (1829)

**Gustave-Gaspard Coriolis**, a French engineer and mathematician, gave work its modern definition:

$$ W = F \times d $$

He introduced the term **"travail"** (work) and the concept of **work done by a force** as the product of force and the distance moved in the direction of the force.

### 🔥 James Prescott Joule (1843–1845)

Joule conducted a famous experiment: a falling weight drove a paddle wheel that stirred water, raising its temperature. He showed that the **same amount of work** always produced the **same amount of heat**:

$$ 4.184 \, \text{J of work} = 1 \, \text{calorie of heat} $$

This established the **mechanical equivalent of heat** and led to the SI unit of work and energy being named the **joule (J)** in his honour.

### 🧠 Hermann von Helmholtz (1847)

In his paper *Über die Erhaltung der Kraft* ("On the Conservation of Force"), Helmholtz unified the concept of work with energy conservation:

> *"The sum of the living forces [kinetic energy] and the tension forces [potential energy] in a system is constant when no external work is done."*

This established work as the **mechanism of energy transfer** — a cornerstone of modern physics.

### 🚀 20th Century & Beyond

- **Einstein (1905)**: work-energy theorem holds relativistically — $W = \Delta E_k$ works at all speeds when mass-energy equivalence is accounted for
- **Quantum mechanics**: work is done by forces at the quantum level, but energy transfer becomes probabilistic and quantised
- **Thermodynamics**: the **first law** ($\Delta U = Q - W$) formalises work as a form of energy transfer in thermodynamic systems

## 📐 Modern Definition

### Work Done

> **Work is done when a force causes an object to move in the direction of the force.** Work is the **transfer of energy** from one form to another.

$$ \boxed{W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos\theta} $$

| Symbol | Meaning | Unit |
|--------|---------|------|
| $W$ | Work done | $\text{J}$ (joule) |
| $F$ | Applied force | $\text{N}$ |
| $d$ | Displacement of the object | $\text{m}$ |
| $\theta$ | Angle between force and displacement | degrees or radians |

### Special Cases

| Situation | $\theta$ | Work Done | Example |
|-----------|---------|-----------|---------|
| Force in direction of motion | $0^\circ$ | $W = F \cdot d$ | Pushing a box forward |
| Force opposite to motion | $180^\circ$ | $W = -F \cdot d$ | Braking, friction opposing motion |
| Force perpendicular to motion | $90^\circ$ | $W = 0$ | Carrying a box horizontally (force upward, motion sideways) |

### Work Done Against Gravity

When lifting an object vertically at constant speed:

$$ W = mgh $$

where $m$ = mass, $g$ = gravitational field strength, $h$ = height lifted.

### Work Done by a Varying Force

When the force varies with distance, work is the **area under a force–displacement (F–d) graph**:

$$ W = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} F(x) \, dx $$

### Work-Energy Theorem

> The net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy:

$$ \boxed{W_{\text{net}} = \Delta E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mu^2} $$

This theorem bridges work and energy — work is the **mechanism** by which energy is transferred.

## 🧪 Key Experiments

### 1. Joule's Paddlewheel Experiment (1843)
A falling weight turns a paddle wheel in a calorimeter of water. Measuring the temperature rise of the water shows that the work done by the falling weight ($mgh$) equals the heat energy gained ($mc\Delta T$). This established the **mechanical equivalent of heat**: $1 \, \text{cal} = 4.184 \, \text{J}$.

### 2. Ramp / Inclined Plane
A mass is pulled up a ramp at constant speed. The work done ($F \times d$) is compared to the gain in gravitational potential energy ($mgh$). Students verify that the **same work** is done regardless of the steepness of the ramp (ignoring friction).

### 3. Spring Force — Work as Area Under F–d Graph
A spring is stretched and the force is recorded at each extension. The work done is the area under the F–d graph ($\frac{1}{2}kx^2$ for an ideal spring). This demonstrates that work done on a spring becomes **elastic potential energy**.

### 4. Work-Energy Theorem with a Trolley
A trolley on a frictionless track is pulled by a constant force over a measured distance. The final kinetic energy is measured using a photogate. The work done ($Fd$) equals $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$ — verifying the work-energy theorem.

## 🌍 Real-World Applications

| Application | How Work Done Matters |
|------------|---------------------|
| 🏋️ **Lifting weights** | Work is done against gravity: $W = mgh$. The same mass lifted higher = more work |
| 🚗 **Braking distance** | Brakes do **negative work** ($W = -F \cdot d$) to remove kinetic energy |
| 🚲 **Cycling uphill** | Rider does work against gravity AND friction. Gears trade force for distance to make work manageable |
| ⚡ **Generators** | Mechanical work (turning a turbine) is converted into electrical energy |
| 🔩 **Hammering a nail** | Work done by the hammer ($F \times d$) transfers kinetic energy into the nail |
| 🪂 **Parachute** | Air resistance does negative work on the skydiver, removing kinetic energy |

## 💡 Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Truth |
|--------------|-------|
| "Holding something heavy = doing work" | ❌ In physics, if there is **no displacement**, no work is done — holding a bag stationary gives $W = 0$ |
| "Work and effort are the same" | ❌ Effort involves internal energy use (muscles, fuel), but work in physics requires **force × displacement** |
| "You always get more work out than you put in with a machine" | ❌ Machines **cannot create work** — work output ≤ work input (energy conservation). Machines only **trade force for distance** |
| "Work done is always positive" | ❌ Work can be **negative** — when force opposes motion (friction, braking), work is negative, removing energy |
| "If no work is done, nothing happens" | ❌ No work = no energy transfer via force × displacement, but other things can happen — changing direction, rotation without translation |
