# ⚛️ Rutherford's Atomic Model — The Discovery of the Nucleus

## 📜 Overview

In **1911**, the New Zealand-born physicist **Ernest Rutherford** (1871–1937) performed the **gold foil experiment** (also called the Geiger–Marsden experiment) at the University of Manchester, which revealed that atoms consist mostly of **empty space** with a tiny, dense, positively charged **nucleus** at the centre.

This was arguably the **single most important experiment in 20th-century physics** — it replaced Thomson's "plum pudding" model with the **nuclear model** of the atom.

> *"It was quite the most incredible event that has ever happened to me in my life. It was almost as incredible as if you fired a 15-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper and it came back and hit you."*
> — **Ernest Rutherford**, describing the alpha-particle backscattering

| Key fact | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| **Who** | Ernest Rutherford (with Hans Geiger & Ernest Marsden) |
| **When** | 1909–1911 (experiment 1909, paper published 1911) |
| **Where** | University of Manchester, England |
| **What** | Fired alpha particles (α) at a thin gold foil → most passed through, but **~1 in 8,000 bounced back** |
| **Why it matters** | Proved atoms have a **nucleus** → founded **nuclear physics** |
| **Nobel** | Rutherford won the **1908 Nobel Prize in Chemistry** (before the gold foil experiment!) — for his work on radioactivity |
| **Model** | **Nuclear model**: tiny positive nucleus (≈ 10⁻¹⁴ m) + orbiting electrons — atom is mostly **empty space** |

## 🔬 The Experiment — Core Content

### The Apparatus

```
Lead box containing radium
    ↓
    ↓ α-particles (He²⁺)
    ↓
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                             │
    │              GOLD FOIL                      │
    │         (≈ 1,000 atoms thick)               │
    │                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
      ↓                 ↓                  ↓
    Most α's            ↓         Most α's pass
    go straight         ↓         through (straight)
    through             ↓
                Some α's deflected
                at small angles
                     ↓
                A few α's bounce
                BACK (≈ 1 in 8,000)
                     ↓
               Zinc sulphide screen
               (flashes when α hits)
```

**Components:**

| Part | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **Radium source** (in lead container) | Emits α-particles (He²⁺ nuclei) at ≈ 2 × 10⁷ m/s |
| **Lead collimator** | Produces a narrow beam of α-particles |
| **Gold foil** | ~0.00006 mm thick (= ~1,000 atoms thick) |
| **Zinc sulphide (ZnS) screen** | α-particles hit → produce tiny **scintillations** (flashes) observable through a microscope |
| **Rotatable microscope** | Could be moved to any angle around the foil to count scintillations |

### What They Observed

| Result | Observation | What it suggested |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Most α-particles** (>99.9%) | Passed straight through with **no deflection** | The atom is mostly **empty space** |
| **Some α-particles** (~0.1%) | Deflected by **small angles** (1°–10°) | A small positive charge inside the atom |
| **Very few α-particles** (~1 in 8,000) | Deflected by **large angles** (>90°) — some **bounced back** (>90°) | A **tiny, massive, positive core** inside the atom! |

> ⚡ **The smoking gun:** The fact that **any** α-particles bounced back was **impossible** in Thomson's plum pudding model. In a uniform positive sphere, the maximum deflection would be tiny — like a rifle bullet hitting a ball of jelly and bouncing back!

## 🧮 The Physics — Why α-particles Bounce

### Rutherford's Reasoning

Thomson's plum pudding predicted that an α-particle passing through a gold atom would be deflected by at most **~0.01°** (if the charge were spread over the whole atom).

Yet Geiger and Marsden found **~1 in 8,000** α-particles were deflected by **>90°** — some even **bounced backward**.

```
In Thomson's model:
    α ● →  ╔══════════════════╗  ← positive charge spread over atom
            ╚══════════════════╝
    Maximum deflection ≈ 0.01°

What actually happened:
    α ● →  ╔══════╗
            ║ (+)  ║  ← tiny nucleus
            ╚══════╝
    At close approach, the strong Coulomb repulsion:
    F = k·q₁·q₂/r²  (when r is very small, F becomes enormous)
    → α-particle can **reverse direction** if it hits the nucleus head-on!
```

### The Calculation

For a head-on collision (exact back-scattering):

The kinetic energy of the α-particle is converted entirely into electric potential energy at the point of closest approach:

```
½ m_α v² = k·q_α·q_Au / r_min

where:
m_α = 6.64 × 10⁻²⁷ kg (α-particle mass)
v = 2 × 10⁷ m/s
q_α = 2e = 2 × 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
q_Au = 79e = 79 × 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²

Solving for r_min:
r_min = k·q_α·q_Au / (½ m_α v²)
     = (8.99×10⁹ × 2 × 79 × (1.602×10⁻¹⁹)²) / (½ × 6.64×10⁻²⁷ × (2×10⁷)²)
     ≈ 2.7 × 10⁻¹⁴ m
```

| Result | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Minimum approach distance** | ≈ 2.7 × 10⁻¹⁴ m |
| **Radius of gold atom** | ≈ 1.4 × 10⁻¹⁰ m |
| **Radius of gold nucleus** | ≈ **7 × 10⁻¹⁵ m** (Rutherford's estimate) |
| **Ratio** | **Nucleus is ~1/10,000 × the size of the atom!** |
| **Fraction of space filled by nucleus** | ≈ (10⁻¹⁴/10⁻¹⁰)³ = **10⁻¹²** — one trillionth! |

## 🔍 Detail — The Rutherford Model (Nuclear Model)

### What Rutherford Proposed

> "The atom consists of a tiny, **positively charged nucleus** (≈ 10⁻¹⁴ m) which contains nearly all the atom's **mass**, surrounded by **electrons** orbiting at relatively large distances (≈ 10⁻¹⁰ m). The atom is mostly **empty space**."

```
              ┌────────────────────────────────┐
              │         Most atoms =            │
              │          empty space            │
              │                                │
              │          . . . . .             │
              │     .              .            │
              │   .    ╔═══╗       .           │
              │   .    ║Nucleus║    .           │
              │   .    ╚═══╝ (+)   .           │
              │     .              .            │
              │          e⁻ . . .              │
              └────────────────────────────────┘
              Atom diameter ~10⁻¹⁰ m (1 Å)
              Nucleus diameter ~10⁻¹⁴ m
              Mass of atom ≈ 99.9% in nucleus
```

### Key Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Nucleus** | Positively charged; contains **protons** (and later neutrons found) |
| **Nucleus size** | ≈ 10⁻¹⁵ to 10⁻¹⁴ m (1–10 fm) |
| **Atom size** | ≈ 10⁻¹⁰ m (1 Å = 100,000 fm) |
| **Mass distribution** | ~99.95%+ of the atom's mass is in the nucleus |
| **Electrons** | Orbit the nucleus (Rutherford was vague about orbits) |
| **Electron arrangement** | Rutherford didn't specify — this was Bohr's contribution (1913) |
| **What holds the atom** | **Coulomb force** (electrostatic attraction between + nucleus and − electrons) |

### What It Got Right

- ✅ The atom has a **dense, positive nucleus** at the centre
- ✅ The atom is mostly **empty space**
- ✅ Most of the **mass** is in the nucleus
- ✅ The **positive charge** is concentrated in the nucleus

### What It Couldn't Explain

| Problem | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| ❓**Stable orbits** | According to Maxwell's equations, an accelerating electron (orbiting) should **radiate EM waves** → lose energy → **spiral into the nucleus in ~10⁻¹¹ s** — atoms shouldn't exist! |
| ❓**Discrete spectra** | Why do atoms emit only **specific colours** (line spectra) and not a continuous rainbow? |
| ❓**Quantisation** | Rutherford's model couldn't explain why electrons only have certain energies |

**Bohr solved this** in 1913 with his **quantum postulates**: electrons can only occupy specific stable orbits (stationary states) and jump between them by emitting/absorbing photons of specific energies.

## 💡 Myth vs Fact

| Myth | Fact |
|------|------|
| Rutherford himself **saw the alpha-particle flashes** | **Geiger and Marsden** did the counting (extremely tedious — ~100 hours of dark-adapted eyes staring at a ZnS screen!). Rutherford provided the theory and direction |
| Rutherford **designed** the gold foil experiment | **Rutherford suggested** the experiment — Geiger and Marsden **conducted** it. Rutherford was famous for assigning tasks to his students |
| The experiment **proved** the nuclear model in one try | They detected **~100 back-scattered α-particles** out of ~800,000 in the initial experiment — it took **months** to convince themselves |
| Thomson's plum pudding model was **obviously wrong** | At the time, the plum pudding model was **widely accepted** — it was the only model that explained the electron. Rutherford's result was **shocking** |
| The gold foil **bounced the α-particles** | The α-particles were **repelled by electrostatic (Coulomb) force** — they never actually touched the nucleus. The bounce was from Coulomb repulsion at very close range |
| Rutherford won the Nobel Prize **for the nuclear model** | He won the **1908 Nobel Prize in Chemistry** (before the gold foil experiment) for his work on radioactivity. The nuclear model never got a second Nobel |
| The nucleus contains **only protons** | Rutherford initially thought the nucleus was made of protons + bound electrons. The **neutron** was discovered by **Chadwick** (Rutherford's student) in **1932** |
| A nucleus is a **solid ball** | Modern understanding: the nucleus is a **quantum many-body system** of protons and neutrons held together by the **strong nuclear force** (not classical billiard balls!) |

## 🎯 Classroom Demonstration

### Analogy 1: The Cricket Ball & Tissue Paper
> Firing α-particles at gold foil is like throwing a cricket ball at a sheet of tissue paper — and sometimes the ball bounces straight back! This was Rutherford's own analogy, though he used a naval shell.

### Analogy 2: The Fly in the Cathedral
> If the nucleus were a **fly** in the centre of a **cathedral**:
> - The cathedral = the atom
> - The fly = the nucleus (tiny, but contains nearly all the mass)
> - The electrons = dust motes orbiting the fly
> - **Everything else = empty space**

### Model Demonstration
> Use a marble (nucleus) at the centre, and roll small ball bearings (α-particles) toward it:
> - Most roll past (no deflection)
> - A few skim past (small deflection)
> - Rarely, a head-on hit → bounces straight back!

### Key Calculations for Students

**1. Nuclear radius from Rutherford scattering:**
Using conservation of energy:
```
r_min = k·q_α·q_Au / (½ m_α v²)
```
This is the **closest approach** — the upper bound on nuclear radius.

**2. Coulomb force at the nuclear surface:**
```
F = k·q_α·q_Au / r²
  = 8.99×10⁹ × 2×79×(1.602×10⁻¹⁹)² / (8×10⁻¹⁵)²
  ≈ 570 N (!!)
```
That's the **electrostatic force** on one α-particle at the nuclear surface — equivalent to lifting a 57 kg weight — acting on a **single subatomic particle**!

### Questions for Students

1. **Why** did most α-particles pass straight through the gold foil?
   - *Answer: The atom is mostly **empty space** — the nucleus is tiny (≈ 1/10,000 of the atom's diameter)*

2. **Why** did some α-particles bounce back?
   - *Answer: A **head-on collision** with the tiny, massive, positively charged **nucleus** — Coulomb repulsion at very close range reverses the α-particle's direction*

3. **What** prediction of the plum pudding model did Rutherford's experiment disprove?
   - *Answer: Thomson's model predicted the positive charge was spread throughout the atom → maximum deflection ≈ 0.01°. Rutherford found **large-angle deflection** → positive charge must be **concentrated** in a tiny nucleus*

4. **Why** couldn't Rutherford's nuclear model explain atomic stability?
   - *Answer: Classical physics predicted the orbiting electron should **radiate energy** and spiral into the nucleus. Bohr later added **quantum postulates** to fix this*

5. **Calculate** the closest approach of an α-particle (4 MeV kinetic energy) to a gold nucleus (Z = 79).
   - *Answer: Use E = k·2e·79e/r → r = 8.99×10⁹ × 2×79×(1.602×10⁻¹⁹)² / (4×10⁶ × 1.602×10⁻¹⁹) ≈ 5.7 × 10⁻¹⁴ m*
