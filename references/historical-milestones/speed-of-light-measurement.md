# 💡 The Measurement of the Speed of Light

## 📜 Overview

The question "How fast does light travel?" puzzled scientists for centuries. Unlike sound or moving objects, light seemed **instantaneous** — no one could detect any delay over terrestrial distances. It took over **2,000 years** of technological progress to move from "it's infinite" to "it's finite but very fast" to the modern value of exactly **299,792,458 m/s**.

> *"Light travels at a finite speed, but it is so fast that the time it takes to cover even vast distances is imperceptible."*
> — Inspired by Galileo's reasoning (1638)

| Milestone | Year | Scientist | Method | Result (m/s) |
|-----------|------|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **First attempt** | ~1600 | Galileo | Lanterns on hills | "Too fast — instantaneous" |
| **First proof (finite)** | **1676** | **Ole Rømer** | Jupiter's moons | ≈ 2.14 × 10⁸ (22% low) |
| **First terrestrial** | 1849 | **Hippolyte Fizeau** | Toothed wheel | 3.15 × 10⁸ |
| **Rotating mirror** | 1850 | **Léon Foucault** | Rotating mirror | 2.98 × 10⁸ |
| **Modern standard** | 1926 | **Albert A. Michelson** | Rotating mirror (Mt. Wilson) | 299,796 ± 4 km/s |
| **SI Definition** | **1983** | — | c = **299,792,458 m/s** (fixed!) | Exact |

## 📅 Timeline of Understanding

| Era | Figure / Work | Contribution |
|-----|---------------|-------------|
| ~350 BCE | **Aristotle** | Believed light was "instantaneous" — no travel time needed |
| ~60 CE | **Hero of Alexandria** | Argued that vision involves light travelling from eye to object — but still instantaneous |
| ~1000 | **Alhazen (Ibn al-Haytham)** | *Book of Optics* — argued light travels at a finite speed (but couldn't measure it) |
| **~1600** | **Galileo Galilei** | **First experimental attempt** — two people with covered lanterns on distant hills → uncovered → measured delay → **too fast to measure** |
| 1638 | Galileo | *Two New Sciences* — concluded light is "if not instantaneous, then extraordinarily fast" |
| **1676** | **Ole Rømer** 🇩🇰 | **First proof of finite speed** — observed timing of Io (Jupiter's moon) systematically changed with Earth's orbital position → delay = light crossing Earth's orbit |
| 1704 | Isaac Newton | *Opticks* — accepted finite speed, ~7–8 minutes for light to travel from Sun to Earth |
| 1728 | **James Bradley** | **Stellar aberration** — stars appear to shift position due to Earth's motion through "light rain" → confirmed Rømer's value independently |
| **1849** | **Hippolyte Fizeau** 🇫🇷 | **First terrestrial measurement** — toothed wheel + rotating mirror between Paris hills (8.6 km round trip) → c = 315,000 km/s |
| **1850** | **Léon Foucault** 🇫🇷 | **Rotating mirror** — improved accuracy → c = 298,000 km/s → also showed light travels **slower in water** (confirming wave theory over Newton's corpuscular theory!) |
| 1862 | Foucault | Improved measurement → c = 299,796 km/s — within 0.2% of modern value |
| 1879 | **Albert A. Michelson** 🇺🇸 | First measurement with rotating mirror (US Naval Academy) → c = 299,910 km/s |
| 1887 | **Michelson & Morley** | **Famous interferometer experiment** — failed to detect "aether wind" → no speed difference in different directions → **no aether exists** (Einstein's relativity born here!) |
| 1905 | **Albert Einstein** | **Special Relativity** — c is the **maximum speed** in the universe; c is the same for **all observers** (constant) |
| 1926 | **Michelson** (Mt. Wilson) | Best pre-electronic measurement: 35 km round trip between Mt. Wilson and Mt. San Antonio → **c = 299,796 ± 4 km/s** |
| 1972 | **Evenson et al.** (NIST) | Laser interferometry + cesium clock → c = **299,792,456.2 ± 1 m/s** |
| **1983** | **17th CGPM** | **c = 299,792,458 m/s is fixed as a defining constant** — the **metre** is now defined from c, not the other way around! |
| 2019 | **SI Redefinition** | c remains fixed (299,792,458 m/s) as one of 7 defining constants of the SI |

## 🔬 The Experiments — Core Content

### Experiment 1: Galileo's Lanterns (~1600)

Galileo and an assistant stood on two distant hills **(~1 km apart)** with covered lanterns. Galileo would uncover his lantern → the assistant would see the light and immediately uncover their lantern → Galileo measured the round-trip delay.

```
Expected delay (for finite c): ~7 μs for 1 km round trip
Human reaction time: ~0.2 s (too slow!)
Result: "if not instantaneous, then extraordinarily fast"
```

> 💡 **Lesson:** Galileo's method was sound — his instruments were just too slow. He concluded correctly that light must be **at least 10× faster than sound**.

---

### Experiment 2: Rømer's Astronomical Method (1676) 🌑

Rømer was observing **Io** (the innermost moon of Jupiter), which orbits Jupiter every ~42.5 hours. He noticed something strange:

| Earth's position | Io eclipse timing | What Rømer saw |
|-----------------|------------------|----------------|
| **Moving toward Jupiter** (Oct–Feb) | **Eclipses occurred earlier** | ~11 minutes early cumulative |
| **Moving away from Jupiter** (Feb–Oct) | **Eclipses occurred later** | ~11 minutes late cumulative |

**Rømer's reasoning:**
- When Earth is **closest** to Jupiter → light has less distance to travel → Io appears to eclipse **earlier**
- When Earth is **farthest** from Jupiter → light has **extra distance** (≈ diameter of Earth's orbit ≈ 2 AU) → Io appears **later**
- Total delay = **~22 minutes** (modern value ≈ 16 min 36 s)

**Calculation:**
```
c = distance / delay
  ≈ (2 × AU) / (22 × 60 s)
  ≈ (2 × 1.5 × 10¹¹ m) / (1,320 s)
  ≈ 2.27 × 10⁸ m/s ≈ 227,000 km/s

Modern value: 299,792 km/s
Rømer was ~25% low — remarkably good for 1676!
```

> **🌟 Significance:** This was the **first quantitative proof** that light travels at a **finite speed**.

---

### Experiment 3: Fizeau's Toothed Wheel (1849) ⚙️

Fizeau used a **toothed wheel** that could spin at up to hundreds of revolutions per second:

**Setup:**
```
Light source → beamsplitter → toothed wheel (spinning)
                               ↓
                          lens collimator
                               ↓
                    8.6 km to a mirror
                               ↓
                          reflected back
                               ↓
                     through the wheel (toothed gap)
                               ↓
                          to observer's eye
```

**Principle:**
1. Start with wheel **stationary** → light passes through a gap → reflects → returns through **same gap** → observer sees light
2. Spin the wheel **faster** → eventually the returning light hits a **tooth** → observer sees **darkness**
3. From the spin speed when darkness occurs → c can be calculated

```
c = (2 × d × N × ω) / θ

where:
d = distance to mirror (8,633 m)
N = number of teeth (720)
ω = rotation speed
θ = angular gap between teeth

First result: c ≈ 315,000 km/s (~5% error)
```

> **🛠️ Innovation:** First successful **terrestrial** measurement — no astronomy needed!

---

### Experiment 4: Foucault's Rotating Mirror (1850) 🔄

Foucault replaced the toothed wheel with a **rotating mirror** — much more precise.

**Setup:**
```
Light source → beamsplitter → spinning mirror → fixed mirror (distant)
                               │                      │
                               │←── 20 m away ──────→│
                               │→ light returns off spinning mirror→
                               ↓
                         observer (sees a deflected spot)
```

**Principle:**
1. Light goes from spinning mirror → fixed mirror → returns
2. **By the time** light returns, the spinning mirror has rotated by a tiny angle (Δθ)
3. The returning beam is deflected by **2 × Δθ**
4. From the deflection + rotation speed + distance:
```
c = (4π × N × d × R) / s

where N = rotations/s, R = distance to fixed mirror, s = spot displacement
```

| Foucault's result | 298,000 km/s (0.6% error) |
|------------------|--------------------------|

**Bonus discovery:** When Foucault placed **water** between the mirrors, he measured c in water = **c/n** (about 225,000 km/s), proving light slows down in a medium — confirming the **wave theory** of light!

---

### Experiment 5: Michelson's Measurements (1879–1926) 🏆

Michelson refined Foucault's rotating mirror method to extraordinary precision.

**Key improvements:**
- **1926: Mt. Wilson → Mt. San Antonio** (35 km round trip — the longest baseline ever)
- Extremely precise rotating mirror (up to 528 rps)
- Vacuum tube between mountains to eliminate air refraction
- Multiple measurements → error bound reduced to **±4 km/s**

```
c = 299,796 ± 4 km/s
Error: ~0.001% — less than 1 part in 100,000!
```

**Michelson's set-up (1926):**
```
Rotating mirror on Mt. Wilson (California)
    ↓
light travels to Mt. San Antonio (Antonio Peak, 35 km away)
    ↓
fixed mirror at Antonio Peak
    ↓
light reflects back to Mt. Wilson
    ↓
return beam shifted by rotation
```

---

### Experiment 6: Modern Measurement — Laser + Atomic Clock (1972) 🎯

By 1972, technology had advanced enough for the ultimate measurement using:

| Tool | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **He-Ne laser** (λ ≈ 3.39 μm) | Extremely stable frequency |
| **Iodine-stabilised laser** | Frequency known to 10⁻¹⁰ |
| **Cesium atomic clock** | Time measured to 10⁻¹³ |
| **Interferometer** | Wavelength measured in terms of Kr-86 standard |

```
c = f × λ

f = laser frequency (≈ 88 THz)
λ = wavelength (≈ 3.39 μm)

Result: c = 299,792,456.2 ± 1.1 m/s
Error: ~1 × 10⁻⁹ (one part in a billion!)
```

## 🔍 Detail — Why Do We Measure c?

### What the speed of light tells us

| Discovery | Implication |
|-----------|-------------|
| c is **finite** | The universe has a **cosmic speed limit** — cause and effect have a delay |
| c is **constant for all observers** | **Special Relativity** — time dilation, length contraction, E=mc² |
| c ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s | Light takes ~8 min from Sun → Earth; ~4 years to nearest star; ~100,000 years across our galaxy |
| No aether was needed | Michelson–Morley (1887): light doesn't need a medium |
| c is the **maximum speed** | Nothing with mass can reach c; only massless particles (photons, gluons) travel at c |

### Modern value
```
c = 299,792,458 m/s (EXACT — since 1983)
```

This is **not measured anymore** — c is a **defining constant** of the SI system. Instead, the **metre** is defined from c and the second.

## 💡 Myth vs Fact

| Myth | Fact |
|------|------|
| Galileo **measured** the speed of light | He **tried** but his instruments were too slow — he correctly concluded it was "extraordinarily fast" |
| Rømer **measured** c | He **proved** c is finite and gave a first estimate, but didn't compute c in modern units — Huygens did that from Rømer's data |
| Fizeau was the **first** to measure c | **First terrestrial** measurement — Rømer was first overall (astronomical) |
| Michelson measured c to find the **aether** | The **null result** of Michelson–Morley was more important than his improved c value — it toppled aether theory |
| The speed of light is **always** c in a medium | c = 299,792,458 m/s is **only** in vacuum. In water, light travels at c/n ≈ 225,000 km/s |
| Einstein **derived** c from relativity | c was **already known** from measurements. Einstein showed it is **constant for all observers** — this was his postulate |
| The speed of light is **slowing down** | No evidence for this (some speculative theories have been proposed but are not mainstream) |
| Light takes **8 minutes** from Sun → Earth | **Correct** — 1 AU / c ≈ 500 s ≈ 8.3 minutes |

## 🎯 Classroom Demonstration — Measuring the Speed of Light

### Microwave + Chocolate 🍫
> The simplest way to measure c at home (or in class):
> 1. Take a chocolate bar and place it in a microwave
> 2. Remove the turntable so the bar stays stationary
> 3. Microwave for ~30 seconds — the chocolate will melt at **hot spots** (antinodes)
> 4. Measure the **distance between melted spots** → this is λ/2
> 5. Most microwaves operate at **2.45 GHz** (check the label)
> 6. c = f × λ = 2.45 × 10⁹ × (2 × spot_spacing)

### Laser and Oscilloscope (Lab)
> A more precise method: pulse a laser → send to a distant mirror → detect the return with a fast photodiode → display on a fast oscilloscope → the time delay between the outgoing and returning pulses = 2d/c.

### Michelson Interferometer (Advanced)
> Split a laser beam → send one arm to a moving mirror → the interference pattern changes as the mirror moves → each fringe = λ/2 displacement → by counting fringes and mirror distance → λ is known → c = fλ.

## 🌍 Applications

| Application | How it relates |
|-------------|---------------|
| 🛰️ **GPS** | Relies on relativistic corrections (time dilation + gravitational time dilation) — would not work without knowing c |
| 🌐 **Fibre optics** | Internet data travels at ~0.67c (in silica fibre) — propagation delay affects global communications |
| 📡 **Radar** | Measures distance by time-of-flight: d = c · Δt / 2 |
| 💻 **Computer chips** | Propagation delay in wires limits clock speed — signal speed in copper = ~0.6c |
| 🚀 **Space communication** | Mars rovers: delay ≈ 4–24 minutes depending on Earth–Mars distance |
| 🔬 **Laser ranging** | Measuring distance to the Moon with ~1 cm accuracy using retroreflectors |
| 🔭 **Astronomy** | We see distant galaxies as they were billions of years ago — c is the "time machine" |
| 📐 **SI definition of the metre** | Since 1983: the metre = distance light travels in 1/299,792,458 s |

### Key Questions

1. **Why didn't Galileo succeed?** — Light is ~10⁶× faster than sound; human reaction time = ~0.2 s; at 1 km distance, light takes only ~3 μs (too fast for 17th-century instruments)

2. **Calculate:** How long does light take to travel from the Sun to Earth?
   - *Answer: t = d/c = 1.496 × 10¹¹ / 2.998 × 10⁸ ≈ 499 s ≈ 8.3 minutes*

3. **Explain** how Rømer proved light has a finite speed.
   - *Answer: Io's eclipses appeared ~22 min earlier when Earth was closest to Jupiter, ~22 min later when farthest — the difference of 2 AU divided by c*

4. **Predict:** If c were 1,000× slower, what would change?
   - *Answer: GPS would be useless; delays in communication would be noticeable even on Earth; relativistic effects would be obvious at everyday speeds*
