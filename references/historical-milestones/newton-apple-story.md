# 🍎 Newton's Apple — The Story of Universal Gravitation

## 📜 Overview

In **1666**, during the **Great Plague** when Cambridge University was closed, the 23-year-old **Isaac Newton** retreated to his family home at **Woolsthorpe Manor** in Lincolnshire. There, according to one of the most famous stories in the history of science, he saw an **apple fall from a tree** — and this simple observation sparked the chain of thought that led to his **law of universal gravitation**.

> *"Why should that apple always descend perpendicularly to the ground? … why should it not go sideways, or upwards? but constantly to the earths centre? assuredly, the reason is, that the earth draws it."*
> — **Isaac Newton**, as recounted by William Stukeley (1752)

## 🗣️ The Original Accounts

### William Stukeley's Account (15 April 1726)

The most reliable account comes from **William Stukeley**, a friend of Newton, who wrote a manuscript memoir in 1752. Stukeley recorded a conversation with the 84-year-old Newton in Kensington:

> *"We went into the garden, & drank thea under the shade of some appletrees, only he, & myself. amidst other discourse, he told me, he was just in the same situation, as when formerly, the notion of gravitation came into his mind.*
>
> *'Why should that apple always descend perpendicularly to the ground,' thought he to himself: occasion'd by the fall of an apple, as he sat in a contemplative mood: 'why should it not go sideways, or upwards? but constantly to the earths centre? assuredly, the reason is, that the earth draws it. there must be a drawing power in matter. & the sum of the drawing power in the matter of the earth must be in the earths center, not in any side of the earth. therefore dos this apple fall perpendicularly, or toward the center. if matter thus draws matter; it must be in proportion of its quantity. therefore the apple draws the earth, as well as the earth draws the apple.'"*

### John Conduitt's Account

**John Conduitt**, Newton's assistant at the Royal Mint and husband of Newton's niece, also recorded the story:

> *"In the year 1666 he retired again from Cambridge to his mother in Lincolnshire. Whilst he was musing in a garden it came into his thought that the same power of gravity (which made an apple fall from the tree to the ground) was not limited to a certain distance from the earth but must extend much further than was usually thought. Why not as high as the Moon? said he to himself."*

### Voltaire's Version (1727)

The story was popularised by **Voltaire**, who heard it from Newton's niece, **Catherine Barton**:

> *"Sir Isaac Newton walking in his gardens, had the first thought of his system of gravitation, upon seeing an apple falling from a tree."*
> — Voltaire, *Essay on Epic Poetry* (1727)

## 🌳 The Original Apple Tree

The apple tree at Woolsthorpe Manor was a **Flower of Kent** variety — a cooking apple now rare in cultivation. The tree:

- Stood in the garden of Woolsthorpe Manor, Newton's birthplace
- Was **blown down in a storm in 1820**
- Parts of the original tree were preserved
- **Descendants/grafits** were propagated and now grow at:
  - Trinity College, Cambridge
  - Cambridge University Botanic Garden
  - Instituto Balseiro, Argentina
  - Various locations worldwide

The tree is still alive today (as a grafted descendant) and is cared for by the **National Trust**, which owns Woolsthorpe Manor. Visitors can see the tree in the same garden where Newton's revelation occurred.

## 🔬 The Scientific Breakthrough

The apple story led Newton to realise that the **same force** that pulls an apple to the ground also holds the Moon in orbit around the Earth. This was the first step toward his **Law of Universal Gravitation**:

$$
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Unit |
|--------|---------|------|
| `F` | Gravitational force between two masses | N |
| `G` | Universal gravitational constant (6.674×10⁻¹¹) | N·m²/kg² |
| `m₁, m₂` | Masses of the two objects | kg |
| `r` | Distance between their centres | m |

### The Moon Test (Newton's "Moon Test")

Newton reasoned:

1. The apple falls with acceleration `g = 9.81 m/s²` at Earth's surface (radius = 6,371 km)
2. The Moon is at distance ≈ 384,400 km ≈ 60 Earth radii
3. If gravity follows an **inverse-square law**: acceleration at Moon's orbit = `g / 60² ≈ 0.0027 m/s²`
4. The Moon's centripetal acceleration toward Earth: `a = v²/r ≈ 0.0027 m/s²` ✓

**The numbers matched.** This confirmed that the same force — gravity — governs both the apple and the Moon.

## 💡 Myth vs Fact

| Myth | Fact |
|------|------|
| 🍎 The apple hit Newton on the head | ❌ **False.** None of the original accounts say the apple hit him. He *saw* it fall. |
| 💡 The discovery was instantaneous | ❌ **False.** Newton worked on the theory for ~20 years before publishing the *Principia* in 1687. |
| 📚 The story was made up by Voltaire | ❌ **Partially false.** Voltaire popularised it, but Newton himself told the story to Stukeley. |
| 🌳 The original tree is still alive | ❌ The original tree blew down in 1820, but **grafted descendants** survive. |
| 🧠 Newton was the only one to think of this | ❌ Robert Hooke also proposed an inverse-square law. Newton's genius was the mathematical proof. |
| 📖 The discovery was published immediately | ❌ Newton delayed publication for 20 years due to fear of controversy. Halley pushed him to publish. |

## 🎯 Classroom Demonstration

### Simple Gravity Thought Experiment

**Materials:**
- An apple (or any small object)
- Your hand

**Procedure:**
1. Hold the apple at shoulder height
2. Drop it — it falls straight down
3. Ask: *"Why does it fall straight down and not sideways or up?"*
4. Discuss: the Earth is pulling it toward its centre
5. Now ask: *"Does this pull stop at the top of the tree? At the clouds? At the Moon?"*

This is exactly the chain of reasoning Newton followed.

### The Moon Test Calculation (for advanced students)

**Given:**
- Moon's orbital radius: `r = 3.84 × 10⁸ m`
- Moon's orbital period: `T = 27.3 days = 2.36 × 10⁶ s`
- Moon's orbital speed: `v = 2πr/T ≈ 1,022 m/s`

**Centripetal acceleration:**
$$a = \frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{(1{,}022)^2}{3.84 \times 10^8} \approx 0.00272 \text{ m/s}^2$$

**Inverse-square prediction:**
$$a = \frac{g}{(r_\text{Moon}/r_\text{Earth})^2} = \frac{9.81}{(60)^2} \approx 0.00273 \text{ m/s}^2$$

**Result:** The two values match within 1% — confirming the inverse-square law! ✓
