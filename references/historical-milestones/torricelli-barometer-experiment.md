# 🧪 Torricelli's Barometer Experiment (1643)

## 📜 Overview

In **1643**, the Italian physicist and mathematician **Evangelista Torricelli** (1608–1647) performed a simple but revolutionary experiment that **proved air has weight** and created the **first barometer** — a device that measures atmospheric pressure. 

The experiment also produced a **stable vacuum** (the Torricellian vacuum) for the first time, challenging the long-held Aristotelian dogma that "nature abhors a vacuum" (*horror vacui*).

> *"We live submerged at the bottom of an ocean of air."*
> — **Evangelista Torricelli**

| Key fact | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| **Who** | Evangelista Torricelli (former student of Benedetto Castelli, colleague of Galileo) |
| **When** | 1643 (Galileo had died in 1642; Torricelli was 35) |
| **Where** | Florence, Italy |
| **What** | Filled a 1-metre glass tube with mercury, inverted it into a dish of mercury → mercury column fell to ~760 mm, leaving a vacuum above |
| **Why it matters** | First quantitative measurement of atmospheric pressure; invention of the barometer; first stable vacuum; refuted Aristotelian physics |

## 🔬 The Experiment — Core Content

### Materials

| Item | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Glass tube | ~1 m long, sealed at one end |
| Mercury (Hg) | Dense liquid metal (~13.6× denser than water) — chosen to keep the tube short |
| Dish | Shallow dish/ basin to hold mercury |
| Cloth or finger | To temporarily seal the open end during inversion |

### Procedure

1. **Fill the tube completely** with mercury — no air bubbles
2. **Seal the open end** with a finger or cloth
3. **Invert the tube** into the dish of mercury
4. **Release the seal** under the mercury surface
5. **Observe** — the mercury column **drops** slightly, then stabilises at a height of about **760 mm** (≈ 76 cm) above the dish surface
6. **The space above** the mercury column = **vacuum** (the "Torricellian vacuum")

### 🔍 Key Observations

| Observation | What it proved |
|-------------|---------------|
| Column height ≈ **760 mm** at sea level | The weight of the atmosphere = weight of a 760 mm column of mercury |
| Column height **varies** with weather | Air pressure changes → weather prediction |
| Column height **decreases** with altitude | (Pascal confirmed this in 1648 — 17 km up Puy-de-Dôme → column dropped ~86 mm) |
| The space above the mercury is **empty** | A **stable vacuum** exists — no "horror vacui"! |
| Tilting the tube → mercury fills the top | The top of the tube was always empty — the space cannot be filled by the mercury even if tilted |

### 🔧 Why Mercury?

Torricelli chose mercury (Hg) for a practical reason:

```
P_atm = ρ_Hg × g × h

h = P_atm / (ρ_Hg × g)
  = 101,325 / (13,546 × 9.81)
  ≈ 0.76 m = 760 mm

If he had used water:
h_water = 101,325 / (1,000 × 9.81) ≈ 10.33 m
```

— A water barometer would need a tube **10.3 metres tall** — impractical for a 17th-century lab!
Mercury is **13.6× denser** → the tube length is only 1/13.6 ≈ **1 metre** — manageable.

> **⚠️ Modern note:** Mercury is highly toxic. Modern barometers use aneroid (sealed metal box) designs or digital sensors instead.

## 🔍 Detail — The Physics Behind It

### What actually happened?

At sea level, the **weight of the atmosphere** pushing down on the mercury dish **supports** the mercury column. The column's weight (per unit area) exactly balances the atmospheric pressure:

```
P_atm = P_Hg column
      = ρ_Hg · g · h
      = 13,546 × 9.81 × 0.760
      ≈ 101,325 Pa
```

The space above the mercury is a **vacuum** because:
- No air was trapped (tube was filled completely)
- No air can get in (open end is submerged in mercury)
- Mercury vapour pressure at room temperature is extremely low (~0.0012 mmHg — negligible)

### Why did it refute Aristotelian physics?

| Aristotle's claim | Torricelli's proof |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| "Nature abhors a vacuum" — vacuum cannot exist | The Torricellian vacuum is **stable and observable** — nature does *not* fill it |
| "Water rises in a pump because nature fills the void" | Water rises because **air pressure** pushes it — this is why the limit is 10.3 m, not infinity |
| "Air has no weight" | The 760 mm mercury column is **supported by air** — air must have weight! |
| "Motion requires a medium" | The vacuum shows that motion (of the falling mercury) can occur without a resisting medium |

## 💡 Myth vs Fact

| Myth | Fact |
|------|------|
| Torricelli invented the **vacuum** | The vacuum had already been created by della Porta (condensing steam). Torricelli's innovation was a **stable, reproducible vacuum** that could be studied |
| Torricelli's experiment was immediately accepted | Many Aristotelian scholars **denied** the vacuum existed — they claimed the space was filled with "subtle matter" (ad hoc hypothesis) |
| Torricelli understood **everything** about atmospheric pressure | He correctly identified **air weight** as the cause but did not fully understand the **variability** of pressure with weather — that came later |
| Pascal thought Torricelli was wrong | Pascal was initially confused but then **proved Torricelli right** with the Puy-de-Dôme experiment (1648) by showing the column height decreases at altitude |
| Mercury is **safe** to handle | Modern scientists know mercury is **highly toxic** (inhaled vapour, skin contact) — Torricelli's generation did not know this |
| The barometer was **immediately used** for weather forecasting | Early users noticed the correlation between column height and weather within a few years — the world's first weather instrument |
| Torricelli worked **alone** | He corresponded with Castelli, Ricci, Pascal, and others — science in the 1600s was already collaborative across Europe |
| Torricelli used water first | Historical evidence suggests he **started with water** (10.3 m tube) then **switched to mercury** for practical reasons |

## 🎯 Classroom Demonstration — Analogies

### Straw & Water Analogy
> Like drinking through a straw — you don't "suck" the liquid up; you lower the pressure in your mouth, and **atmospheric pressure** pushes the liquid up. The theoretical maximum height for water with a perfect vacuum = 10.3 m.

### Can Crush Demo
> Heat a small amount of water in an aluminium can → invert into cold water → steam condenses → **pressure inside drops** → atmospheric pressure **crushes** the can instantly. Dramatic proof of air pressure.

### Magdeburg Hemispheres (von Guericke, 1654)
> Two metal hemispheres fit together tightly → air pumped out → **two teams of 8 horses** cannot pull them apart! The force holding them = P_atm × A = 101,325 × π(0.3)² ≈ 28,000 N (≈ weight of ~3 tonnes).

## 🌍 Applications

| Application | How it relates |
|-------------|---------------|
| 🌦️ **Weather forecasting** | Barometric pressure trends → predict storms (falling = bad, rising = good) |
| ✈️ **Altimeter in aircraft** | Pressure decreases with altitude → calibrated to show height |
| 🫁 **Respiratory physiology** | Breathing = pressure difference; mechanical ventilation uses positive pressure |
| 💉 **Syringes / IV drips** | Pressure difference moves fluids — same principle as Torricelli |
| 🏗️ **Water supply systems** | Water towers use gravity and atmospheric pressure to supply buildings; max height ≈ 10.3 m if only using atmospheric pressure |
| 🌊 **Submarine / SCUBA** | Understanding hydrostatic pressure (P = ρgh) and absolute pressure |
| 🧪 **Pressure measurement** | All modern pressure sensors (strain gauge, capacitive, piezoelectric) descend from Torricelli's insight |

### Questions for Students

1. **Calculate:** What is the pressure of the atmosphere if a mercury barometer reads 740 mm? (ρ_Hg = 13,600 kg/m³, g = 9.81 m/s²)
   - *Answer: P = 13,600 × 9.81 × 0.740 ≈ 98,700 Pa*

2. **Explain:** Why does a barometer reading decrease when you climb a mountain?
   - *Answer: Less air above → lower atmospheric pressure → less force pushing on the mercury dish → column falls*

3. **Compare:** Why did Torricelli use mercury instead of water?
   - *Answer: Mercury is 13.6× denser → tube length = 1/13.6 of water (1 m vs 10.3 m)*

4. **Predict:** What would happen to the mercury column if you tilted the tube?
   - *Answer: The vertical height remains the same (760 mm); the top of the tube still shows vacuum*
