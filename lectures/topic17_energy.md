## 📜 History

The concept of **energy** evolved slowly through the 19th century as physicists realised that heat, motion, electricity, and chemical reactions were all different forms of the same thing.

**James Prescott Joule (1818–1889)**, an English brewer and physicist, conducted meticulous experiments showing that mechanical work could be converted into heat. His famous experiment involved a falling weight turning a paddle wheel in water — the friction heated the water. This established the **mechanical equivalent of heat**.

**Key figures:**
- **Galileo & Newton** — understood kinetic energy ($\frac{1}{2}mv^2$)
- **Thomas Young** (1807) — first used the word "energy" in the modern scientific sense
- **Joule** (1840s) — established the relationship between work and heat
- **Hermann von Helmholtz** (1847) — formalised the **Law of Conservation of Energy**

> **Myth vs Fact:** Perpetual motion machines (devices that produce more energy than they consume) are impossible. The First Law of Thermodynamics (Conservation of Energy) means energy cannot be created or destroyed — only transformed. Every proposed perpetual motion machine either stops or violates this law.

## 🌍 Nature & Applications

- **🔋 Batteries**: Convert chemical energy → electrical energy
- **☀️ Solar panels**: Convert light energy → electrical energy
- **🧲 Generators**: Convert kinetic energy → electrical energy
- **🍔 Food**: Chemical energy in food → thermal energy (warmth) + kinetic energy (movement)
- **🚗 Electric cars**: Battery chemical energy → electrical → kinetic (motion)
- **🌊 Hydroelectric dams**: Gravitational potential energy → kinetic → electrical
- **🪁 Wind turbines**: Kinetic energy of wind → kinetic energy of blades → electrical energy

## ⚛️ Theory

### Energy

**Energy** is the capacity to do work. SI unit: **joule (J)**.

**Principle of Conservation of Energy:**

> Energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be transferred from one form to another.

$$ E_{\text{total before}} = E_{\text{total after}} $$

### Forms of Energy

| Form | Symbol | Description | Example |
|------|--------|-------------|---------|
| Kinetic | $E_k$ | Energy of motion | Moving car, wind |
| Gravitational potential | $E_p$ or $E_g$ | Energy due to height | Raised weight, water behind a dam |
| Elastic potential | $E_e$ | Energy stored in stretched/compressed material | Drawn bow, compressed spring |
| Chemical | $E_c$ | Energy stored in chemical bonds | Food, fuel, battery |
| Thermal | $Q$ or $E_{th}$ | Energy due to temperature | Hot objects |
| Electrical | $E_e$ | Energy of moving charges | Current in a wire |
| Nuclear | $E_n$ | Energy stored in the nucleus | Fission, fusion |
| Sound | $E_s$ | Energy of vibrating air | Music, speech |
| Light | $E_l$ | Radiant energy | Sun, light bulbs |

### Kinetic Energy

$$ E_k = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 $$

Where:
- $E_k$ = kinetic energy (J)
- $m$ = mass (kg)
- $v$ = velocity (m/s)

### Gravitational Potential Energy

$$ E_p = mgh $$

Where:
- $E_p$ = gravitational potential energy (J)
- $m$ = mass (kg)
- $g$ = gravitational field strength (N/kg)
- $h$ = height above a reference point (m)

### Work

**Work** is done when a force causes an object to move in the direction of the force.

$$ W = Fd $$

Where:
- $W$ = work done (J)
- $F$ = force (N)
- $d$ = distance moved in the direction of the force (m)

**Key idea**: $1 \text{ J}$ of work is done when a force of $1 \text{ N}$ moves an object $1 \text{ m}$.

$$ 1 \text{ J} = 1 \text{ Nm} $$

### Power

**Power** is the rate at which work is done (or energy is transferred).

$$ P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{E}{t} $$

Where:
- $P$ = power (W)
- $W$ = work done (J)
- $E$ = energy transferred (J)
- $t$ = time taken (s)

$$ 1 \text{ W} = 1 \text{ J/s} $$

Alternative formula for power (using force and velocity):

$$ P = Fv $$

Where $v$ = constant velocity (m/s) — useful for vehicles climbing hills or overcoming drag.

### Energy Transfers (Sankey Diagrams)

A **Sankey diagram** shows the flow of energy through a system — the width of each arrow is proportional to the amount of energy.

```
        ┌──────────┐
        │           │──→ Useful energy (e.g., kinetic)
Input ──→  Device   │
        │           │──→ Wasted energy (e.g., heat from friction)
        └──────────┘
```

**Efficiency**:

$$ \text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful output energy}}{\text{total input energy}} \times 100\% $$

Efficiency can also be calculated using power:

$$ \text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful power output}}{\text{total power input}} \times 100\% $$

## ✍️ Worked Examples

### Example 1: Kinetic Energy

A car of mass 1200 kg travels at 20 m/s. What is its kinetic energy?

**Solution:**
$$ E_k = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 1200 \times 20^2 $$
$$ = 600 \times 400 = 240\,000 \text{ J} = 240 \text{ kJ} $$

---

### Example 2: Gravitational Potential Energy

A 5 kg book is lifted from the floor to a shelf 2 m high. How much GPE does it gain? ($g = 9.8$ N/kg)

**Solution:**
$$ E_p = mgh = 5 \times 9.8 \times 2 = 98 \text{ J} $$

---

### Example 3: Work Done

A person pushes a box with a force of 50 N over a distance of 6 m. How much work is done?

**Solution:**
$$ W = Fd = 50 \times 6 = 300 \text{ J} $$

---

### Example 4: Power

A motor does 2400 J of work in 60 seconds. What is its power?

**Solution:**
$$ P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{2400}{60} = 40 \text{ W} $$

---

### Example 5: Efficiency

A light bulb receives 100 J of electrical energy. It produces 10 J of light energy and 90 J of thermal energy.

**(a)** What is the efficiency?
**(b)** What is the percentage efficiency?

**Solution (a):**
$$ \text{Efficiency} = \frac{10}{100} = 0.1 $$

**Solution (b):**
$$ \text{Efficiency} = 0.1 \times 100\% = 10\% $$

---

### Example 6: Conservation of Energy (Roller Coaster)

A roller coaster car of mass 500 kg is at the top of a 30 m high hill. It rolls down (ignore friction).

**(a)** What is its GPE at the top?
**(b)** What is its speed at the bottom?

**Solution (a):**
$$ E_p = mgh = 500 \times 9.8 \times 30 = 147\,000 \text{ J} $$

**Solution (b):**
At the bottom, all GPE has been converted to KE:
$$ \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = mgh $$
$$ \frac{1}{2} v^2 = gh $$
$$ v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.8 \times 30} $$
$$ v = \sqrt{588} \approx 24.2 \text{ m/s} $$
