## 📜 History

The concepts of mass and weight were revolutionised by **Sir Isaac Newton** in the 17th century.

Before Newton, people used the terms "mass" and "weight" interchangeably. In everyday life, this still happens — we say "this weighs 5 kg" when we really mean "its mass is 5 kg".

**Newton's Second Law** ($F = ma$) provided the first clear distinction:
- **Mass** is a measure of the amount of matter in an object — it resists changes in motion (inertia)
- **Weight** is the gravitational force acting on that mass

**The kilogram** was originally defined (1795) as the mass of one litre of water. In 1889, it was replaced by a platinum-iridium cylinder — "Le Grand K". In **2019**, the kilogram was redefined in terms of Planck's constant ($h$), linking it to fundamental physics instead of a physical object.

> **Myth vs Fact:** An astronaut in space is NOT "weightless" because they have no mass. They still have mass — they are weightless because they are in **free fall** around Earth. The ISS and everything inside it is falling towards Earth constantly, but moving sideways so fast they keep missing it!

## 🌍 Nature & Applications

- **🪐 Why does the Moon orbit Earth?** Gravity provides the centripetal force
- **⚖️ Spring balances** measure weight (force), not mass
- **📱 Digital scales** typically measure the force and divide by $g$ to display mass
- **🚀 Rocket launches** must account for the changing weight as fuel burns and gravity decreases with height
- **🏋️ On different planets**, your mass stays the same but your weight changes dramatically

## ⚛️ Theory

### Mass

- **Definition**: The amount of matter in an object
- **SI unit**: kilogram (kg)
- **Scalar** quantity (has magnitude only, no direction)
- **Does NOT change** with location — your mass is the same on Earth, the Moon, or in deep space
- **Measured with**: a top-pan balance or a beam balance (comparison against known masses)

### Weight

- **Definition**: The gravitational force exerted on an object
- **SI unit**: newton (N)
- **Vector** quantity (direction: towards the centre of the gravitating body)
- **DOES change** with location — your weight on the Moon is about $\frac{1}{6}$ of your weight on Earth
- **Measured with**: a spring balance (or newton-meter / force meter)

### The Equation

$$ W = mg $$

Where:
- $W$ = weight (N)
- $m$ = mass (kg)
- $g$ = gravitational field strength (N/kg)

On Earth, $g \approx 9.8$ N/kg. **This is the same number** as the acceleration due to gravity ($9.8$ m/s²) — because:
$$ 1 \text{ N/kg} = 1 \text{ m/s}^2 $$

### Gravitational Field Strength

| Celestial Body | $g$ (N/kg) |
|---------------|-----------|
| Earth | 9.8 |
| Moon | 1.6 |
| Mars | 3.7 |
| Jupiter | 24.8 |
| Sun | 274 |
| Deep space | 0 (weightless) |

### Difference Between Mass and Weight

| Property | Mass | Weight |
|----------|------|--------|
| Definition | Amount of matter | Gravitational force |
| SI unit | kilogram (kg) | newton (N) |
| Quantity type | Scalar | Vector |
| Changes with location? | No | Yes |
| Measured by | Balance | Spring balance |
| Symbol | $m$ | $W$ |

### Inertia

**Inertia** is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its motion. It is directly proportional to mass:
- A heavy truck has **more inertia** — harder to start moving, harder to stop
- A tennis ball has **less inertia** — easy to throw, easy to catch

$$ F = ma \quad \text{(more mass → more force needed to accelerate)} $$

## ✍️ Worked Examples

### Example 1: Weight on Different Planets

An astronaut has a mass of 75 kg.

**(a)** What is their weight on Earth ($g = 9.8$ N/kg)?
**(b)** What is their weight on the Moon ($g = 1.6$ N/kg)?
**(c)** What is their mass on the Moon?

**Solution (a):**
$$ W = mg = 75 \times 9.8 = 735 \text{ N} $$

**Solution (b):**
$$ W = mg = 75 \times 1.6 = 120 \text{ N} $$

**Solution (c):**
Mass does not change — still **75 kg** on the Moon.

---

### Example 2: Finding Mass from Weight

A bag of rice has a weight of 49 N on Earth. What is its mass?

**Solution:**
$$ m = \frac{W}{g} = \frac{49}{9.8} = 5.0 \text{ kg} $$

---

### Example 3: Comparing Inertia

Two identical trolleys are pushed with the same force. Trolley A has a 2 kg mass on top, Trolley B has a 5 kg mass. Which accelerates more slowly? Explain.

**Solution:**
Trolley B accelerates more slowly because it has greater mass, therefore greater inertia. From $a = \frac{F}{m}$, for the same force, a larger mass gives a smaller acceleration.
