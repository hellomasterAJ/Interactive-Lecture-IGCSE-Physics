📜 📜 History

The concepts of mass and weight were revolutionised by Sir Isaac Newton in the 17th century.

Before Newton, people used the terms "mass" and "weight" interchangeably. In everyday life, this still happens — we say "this weighs 5 kg" when we really mean "its mass is 5 kg".

Newton's Second Law () provided the first clear distinction:
- Mass is a measure of the amount of matter in an object — it resists changes in motion (inertia)
- Weight is the gravitational force acting on that mass

The kilogram was originally defined (1795) as the mass of one litre of water. In 1889, it was replaced by a platinum-iridium cylinder — "Le Grand K". In 2019, the kilogram was redefined in terms of Planck's constant (), linking it to fundamental physics instead of a physical object.

Myth vs Fact: An astronaut in space is NOT "weightless" because they have no mass. They still have mass — they are weightless because they are in free fall around Earth. The ISS and everything inside it is falling towards Earth constantly, but moving sideways so fast they keep missing it!

⚛️ ⚛️ Theory

Mass

  • Definition: The amount of matter in an object
  • SI unit: kilogram (kg)
  • Scalar quantity (has magnitude only, no direction)
  • Does NOT change with location — your mass is the same on Earth, the Moon, or in deep space
  • Measured with: a top-pan balance or a beam balance (comparison against known masses)

Weight

  • Definition: The gravitational force exerted on an object
  • SI unit: newton (N)
  • Vector quantity (direction: towards the centre of the gravitating body)
  • DOES change with location — your weight on the Moon is about of your weight on Earth
  • Measured with: a spring balance (or newton-meter / force meter)

Inertia

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its motion. It is directly proportional to mass:
- A heavy truck has more inertia — harder to start moving, harder to stop
- A tennis ball has less inertia — easy to throw, easy to catch

$$W = mg$$
$$1 \text{ N/kg} = 1 \text{ m/s}^2$$
$$F = ma \quad \text{(more mass → more force needed to accelerate)}$$
