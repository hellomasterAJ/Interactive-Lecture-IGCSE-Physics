📜 📜 History

The scientific study of forces began with Galileo Galilei (1564–1642) and was revolutionised by Sir Isaac Newton (1643–1727).

Aristotle believed that a force was needed to keep an object moving — if you stop pushing, it stops moving. This seems intuitive, but it's wrong (it ignores friction).

Galileo's thought experiment: Imagine a ball rolling down one inclined plane and up another. The smoother the surfaces, the closer it gets to its original height. If there's no friction at all, the ball would roll forever. This led to the concept of inertia.

Newton synthesised these ideas into his Three Laws of Motion, published in 1687 in Principia Mathematica:
1. An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion, unless acted on by a net external force
2. — force equals mass times acceleration
3. For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction

Robert Hooke (1635–1703) discovered the relationship between force and extension in elastic materials — Hooke's Law — during his work as Curator of Experiments for the Royal Society.

Myth vs Fact: Newton didn't discover gravity when an apple hit his head. The apple story is a metaphor — he saw an apple fall and wondered whether the same force that pulls apples to Earth could extend to the Moon.

🌍 🌍 Nature & Applications

  • 🚗 Seatbelts: Use Newton's First Law — without a seatbelt, your body continues moving forward when the car stops suddenly
  • 🏋️ Weightlifting: — the force needed to lift a barbell depends on its mass and acceleration
  • 🎱 Billiards/Pool: Action-reaction pairs — the cue ball exerts a force on the object ball, which exerts an equal force back
  • 🔧 Springs: Hooke's Law governs suspension systems, mattresses, trampolines, and weighing scales
  • 🪂 Parachutes: Air resistance (drag force) opposes weight, creating terminal velocity
  • 🚀 Rocket propulsion: Action-reaction — exhaust gases push down, rocket pushes up

⚛️ ⚛️ Theory

What is a Force?

A force is a push, pull, or twist that can change the motion or shape of an object.

  • SI unit: newton (N)
  • Vector quantity (has magnitude and direction)
  • Measured with a spring balance (force meter / newton-meter)

Newton's Laws

First Law (Law of Inertia):

An object remains at rest or in uniform motion (constant velocity) unless acted on by a net external force.

Second Law ():

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force and inversely proportional to its mass.

Third Law (Action-Reaction):

When object A exerts a force on object B, object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

Note: The two forces act on different objects, so they don't "cancel out".

Hooke's Law

For an elastic material (like a spring), the extension is directly proportional to the applied force, up to the limit of proportionality (elastic limit).

Where:
- = applied force (N)
- = spring constant (N/m) — a measure of stiffness
- = extension or compression (m)

Stiffer springs have a larger value.

Elastic limit: If stretched beyond this point, the material becomes permanently deformed (does not return to its original shape).

Example 4: Newton's Third Law

A person pushes against a wall with a force of 50 N. Explain what happens.

Solution:
The wall pushes back on the person with an equal force of 50 N in the opposite direction. The person doesn't move because the reaction force from the wall balances their push (or friction from the ground prevents them from sliding). The two forces act on different objects: the 50 N push acts on the wall, the 50 N reaction acts on the person.

$$a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{200}{50} = 4.0 \text{ m/s}^2$$
$$x = 6 \text{ cm} = 0.06 \text{ m}$$
$$k = \frac{F}{x} = \frac{15}{0.06} = 250 \text{ N/m}$$
$$E_e = \frac{1}{2} k x^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 200 \times (0.1)^2$$
